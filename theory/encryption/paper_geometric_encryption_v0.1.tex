\begin{abstract}
\noindent We introduce \emph{geometric encryption}, a
property-preserving database-encryption framework in which
encryption is modeled as a gauge action on the fibers of a
fiber-bundle data store. The central theorem characterizes the
ciphertext-computable query class: a query is computable on
encrypted data at native server speed with closed-form client
post-processing iff its answer space carries an efficiently
invertible action $\rho_g$ compatible with the gauge. The
gauge-invariant slice (curvature, spectral gap, Betti numbers,
mean holonomy) requires no post-processing; the non-trivial
equivariant slice --- the twelve standard analytical-SQL
aggregates on affine ciphertext --- admits closed-form client
recovery from a single ciphertext-side server computation. We
instantiate the framework with five encryption modes (Affine,
Opaque, Indexed, Probabilistic, Isometric) and provide an
explicit per-mode leakage profile graded by the Chase--Kamara
structured-encryption taxonomy. A reference implementation in
Rust ($1000+$ passing tests) and an independent Python
math-validation suite ($68+$ tests across three oracle files)
confirm the algebraic invariance claims; the higher-level
cryptographic constructions, the per-mode security analyses, and
the empirical evaluation are developed in a companion preprint.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{The compute-on-ciphertext problem}
\label{sec:intro:problem}

Every database in production today asks its operators to choose
between confidentiality and computation. Encrypt the data and you
cannot query it without first decrypting on a privileged node; query
it natively and you have surrendered confidentiality to whoever
controls that node. Three decades after Rivest, Adleman, and
Dertouzos asked whether the choice was forced~\cite{gentry2009fhe},
the dominant answers cluster into two families with sharp
trade-offs:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Fully homomorphic encryption} (Gentry 2009 and
  successors~\cite{gentry2009fhe,bgv2012,ckks2017}) provides universality
  at a $10^3$--$10^5\times$ slowdown. Any function on plaintext can be
  evaluated on ciphertext, at a cost that has slowly come down but
  remains incompatible with production latency budgets for analytical
  queries.
\item \textbf{Transparent data encryption} (Oracle TDE, AWS RDS
  encryption, MongoDB CSFLE) provides at-rest confidentiality at a
  $1\times$ cost --- but requires decryption to compute. Every
  analytical query passes through plaintext at some point on a
  privileged node.
\end{enumerate}

Between these poles sits a smaller family of \emph{property-preserving
encryption} (PPE) constructions: order-preserving
encryption~\cite{boldyreva2009ope,popa2011cryptdb}, structured
encryption~\cite{chase2010structured}, searchable symmetric
encryption~\cite{curtmola2006sse}, and functional
encryption~\cite{boneh2011fe}. Each preserves one specific property
(order, equality, a chosen function class). None offer a unifying
framework that says ``encryption mode $X$ preserves query class $Y$
because of mathematical reason $Z$.'' We provide such a framework.
The closest spiritual antecedent in the cryptographic literature is
\emph{privacy-preserving blueprints}~\cite{kohlweiss2023blueprints},
which prove that committed values satisfy a stated policy without
revealing the values themselves; the gauge-invariant ring developed
in \S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance} is the database-runtime
instantiation of the same idea, with the structure group of the
encryption playing the role of the policy.

\emph{In this paper, we propose \textbf{geometric encryption} --- a
framework in which the choice of encryption mode and the choice of
ciphertext-computable query class are two views of the same
mathematical object: the structure group $G$ acting on the bundle's
fiber. The framework recovers several standard
property-preserving patterns --- order, equality, distance, and
selected aggregate computation --- as instances of a single
equivariance principle (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance}) and
adds, beyond them, a large new equivariant query class on which
any computation runs at native server speed without ever
decrypting.}

\subsection{The geometric move}
\label{sec:intro:geometric}

Consider a research hospital shipping de-identified patient records
to an external statistics lab for population-level analytics ---
disease prevalence, treatment-response curves, drug-interaction
warnings. Today the hospital encrypts the records at rest with
TDE; when the lab requests a query, a privileged node decrypts the
relevant columns, computes the answer, and ships it back. The lab
gets a number. The privileged operator sees every record. The
cryptographic primitive that lets the lab compute population-level
statistics without anyone, anywhere, holding decrypted patient
data has remained a long-standing target of property-preserving database
encryption for two decades. The geometric move is the observation
that the framework's analytics already \emph{are} that primitive
--- if the database is presented as a fiber bundle.

The Davis framework~\cite{davis2026sheafcomposition,davis2026manifold}
represents a database as sections of a fiber bundle
$(E, B, F, \pi, \Phi)$, where $B$ is the base space (keys), $F$ is the
fiber (non-key field values), and $\pi: E \to B$ is the projection. A
record is a section $\sigma: p \mapsto (p, v_1, \ldots, v_k) \in E$.

The framework's analytics --- curvature, anomaly detection, spectral
connectivity, prediction --- are functions on the bundle's intrinsic
geometry. Crucially, they are \emph{invariants} of the bundle's
geometric structure: they do not depend on which coordinate system the
fiber is expressed in.

Geometric encryption is the observation that the choice of coordinate
system on the fiber \emph{is} the encryption. A secret gauge
transformation $g \in G \subset \mathrm{Aut}(F)$ scrambles each
record's fiber values $(v_1, \ldots, v_k) \mapsto g(v_1, \ldots, v_k)$.
An attacker who sees only the gauged data cannot recover plaintext
(without knowing $g$); but anyone --- including analysts without
decryption privilege --- can compute any $G$-invariant function on the
gauged data and get the same answer as on plaintext.

The encryption is not a layer bolted onto the database; it is a
gauge action on the same geometric object the engine already
uses for its analytics. \textbf{The geometry is the encryption.}

\subsection{What this paper adds}
\label{sec:intro:adds}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{We give a closed taxonomy} of five encryption modes
  (Affine, Opaque, Indexed, Probabilistic, Isometric) keyed by their
  structure groups. Each row of the taxonomy is a triple (structure
  group $G$, AEAD/PRF primitive, query class preserved).
\item \textbf{We provide eight v0.3 cryptographic constructions}:
  Curvature-MAC bundle integrity (Sprint~I), capability delegation
  with four interoperable modes
  ($\mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})$ algebraic, Sprint~J;
  BLS12-381 pairing-based, Sprint~J.2;
  ML-KEM-768 trusted-delegatee, Sprint~J.3;
  lattice threshold, Sprint~J.4),
  the holonomy ledger (Sprint~K, RFC 6962 Merkle audit log),
  \v{C}ech threshold sharing (Sprint~L), and a continuous RG-flow
  ratchet for per-write forward secrecy (Sprint~M).
\item \textbf{We provide four v0.4 cryptographic constructions}:
  public deterministic verification of the invariant fingerprint
  $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ from ciphertext alone, with no gauge key
  given to the verifier (Sprint~N);
  HMAC-bound credential-gated invariant queries with a runtime
  falsification harness that re-evaluates ad-hoc query callbacks
  under five random gauges and rejects anything not in the
  invariant ring $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Aff}}$ (Sprint~O);
  a geodesic-ball membership index with dimension-aware
  $\chi^2(k, 1-\alpha)$ threshold and per-field-gauge ellipsoidal
  variant (Sprint~P); and a characterization of the
  $K$-preserving transformation group as the diagonal-affine
  semidirect product $G_{\mathrm{AffK}} = (\mathbb{R}^*)^k \ltimes
  \mathbb{R}^k$ (Sprint~Q).
\item \textbf{We provide a reference implementation}: the public
  GIGI Rust codebase~\cite{gigi2026code} carries a TDD test
  surface ($1000+$ passing tests), with each math primitive
  backed by both a Rust integration test and an independent
  Python math-validation mirror.
\item \textbf{We document the honest scope statements} for each
  construction's threat model. $\mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})$ capability delegation
  (\S 6) is \emph{not} collusion-resistant PRE in the
  Ateniese--Hohenberger sense~\cite{ateniese2005pre,libert2008pre} ---
  a fundamental consequence of additive arithmetic. The
  $(\alpha, \beta)$ capability plus the delegatee's own gauge key
  yields a 2-equations-in-2-unknowns linear system whose solution
  is the delegator's secret in $O(1)$ field operations. This means
  Aff$(\mathbb{R})$ delegation requires \emph{both} the delegatee
  being honest \emph{and} the delegatee's key remaining
  uncompromised; a key-leak in the delegatee compounds with a
  capability leak to immediately expose the delegator.
\item For deployments where Alice wants to delegate decryption to
  a specific Bob without ever exposing $sk_A$ in plaintext, we
  provide a pairing-based \emph{bearer-capability delegation}
  primitive in
  \texttt{src/pairing\_delegation.rs}~\cite{gigi2026code}, related
  in spirit to Umbral-style PRE constructions like
  Umbral~\cite{nunez2018umbral} but simpler in shape. The
  construction is conceptually adjacent to two decades of
  anonymous-credential delegation work originating with the CL
  signature scheme~\cite{camenisch2004clsigs} and continuing
  through mercurial
  signatures~\cite{camenisch2019mercurial, garman2024mercurial};
  we deliver a database-record-access analogue at the storage
  layer. Construction: BLS12-381 keypairs $sk \in \mathbb{F}_p$,
  $pk = g_2^{sk} \in G_2$; KEM capsule $C_1 = g_1^r \in G_1$;
  delegation capability
  $rk_{A \to B} = pk_B^{1/sk_A} = g_2^{sk_B / sk_A} \in G_2$. To
  decrypt a capsule, the bearer of $rk$ computes
  \[
  K_{\mathrm{shared}} \;=\; e(C_1,\, rk_{A \to B})
                       \;=\; e(g_1, g_2)^{r \cdot sk_B / sk_A},
  \]
  and derives an AEAD key from $K_{\mathrm{shared}}$ via
  HKDF-SHA256. Alice can derive the same $K_{\mathrm{shared}}$
  herself from $sk_A$ and $pk_B$ without materializing $rk$,
  enabling sender--receiver agreement.
  \par
  \par
  \textbf{Scope of the security claim.} This construction provides
  \emph{$rk$-unforgeability without $sk_A$}: an adversary holding
  the public parameters $(G_1, G_2, G_T, e, g_1, g_2, pk_A, pk_B)$
  but \emph{not} $rk_{A \to B}$ cannot derive a valid delegation
  token. Constructing $rk = g_2^{sk_B / sk_A}$ from
  $(pk_A, pk_B)$ alone is an exponent-ratio problem in $G_2$
  --- given $(g^a, g^b)$, compute $g^{b/a}$ --- closely related
  to the inverse Diffie--Hellman family. It is efficiently
  solvable given discrete logarithms (hard at ${\sim}2^{128}$
  work in BLS12-381) and is plausibly hard in the generic group
  model, but we do not establish a reduction in either direction
  here; we treat it as a bearer-capability hardness assumption
  rather than a full PRE security proof. We emphasize
  \emph{this is not} strict collusion-resistant proxy
  re-encryption in the Ateniese--Hohenberger
  sense~\cite{ateniese2005pre,libert2008pre}: $rk$ is a bearer
  capability, and a coalition that holds both $rk$ and any
  visible capsule $C_1$ derives $K_{\mathrm{shared}}$ directly
  via the public pairing, with no additional hardness assumption
  needed. The appropriate threat model is therefore
  honest-delegatee: Alice transmits $rk$ to Bob through a
  \emph{confidential and authenticated} channel (authentication
  alone is insufficient because anyone who observes $rk$ can use
  it), and the security perimeter is the set of parties Bob
  (and any proxy he chooses) intentionally shares $rk$ with. The full Ateniese--Hohenberger AHP construction
  with a separate $g_1^r$-bound commitment encrypted under Bob's
  public key --- which would bind capsule-use to $sk_B$ and
  achieve collusion-resistance against the (proxy~$\cup$~$rk$)
  coalition --- is the open successor construction; we discuss
  the design space in the companion preprint (\S 6) alongside the
  ML-KEM trusted-delegatee mode below and the lattice threshold
  construction that achieves true information-theoretic
  collusion-resistance in the multi-party setting.
\item \textbf{Three derived-but-unimplemented constructions}
  (spectral-signature ZKP, lattice-fiber PQ gauge, full
  Sigma-protocol upgrade of the deterministic verifier) with
  security arguments, deferred to a successor preprint pending
  peer review of the construction.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope: what this paper is and is not}
\label{sec:intro:not}

\paragraph{Affine aggregate coverage.}
Geometric encryption achieves plaintext-exact, native-speed
recovery for the standard analytical-SQL aggregate class on
ciphertext: COUNT, SUM, AVG, VAR, STDDEV, MIN, MAX, RANGE, MEDIAN,
QUANTILE, ARGMIN, ARGMAX --- twelve aggregate operators --- plus
any polynomial combination of these and GROUP BY / HAVING over
them. The server evaluates the aggregate directly on ciphertext;
the client recovers the plaintext result via a single closed-form
$\rho_g^{-1}$ application per query, at cost $O(\mathrm{poly}(m))$
in the answer dimension $m$ and independent of the record count.
The record set never leaves ciphertext form on the server. This is
the surface on which the framework covers a complementary
aggregate class at native database cost under an explicit
leakage profile (\S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}), in
contrast with fully homomorphic
encryption~\cite{gentry2009fhe,bgv2012,ckks2017}, which targets
arbitrary-function evaluation at $10^3$ to $10^5\times$ overhead
under bounded-depth and ciphertext-blow-up restrictions. Range queries (via
literal-transform on Affine), equality search (via INDEXED's
deterministic PRF), distance and $k$-NN (via ISOMETRIC's
orthogonal action on grouped numeric fields), and $\sigma$-bucket
approximate equality (via PROBABILISTIC plus the Davis Identity
$S + d^2 = 1$, which makes equality on the noised representation
a distance check) sit alongside the aggregates and round out the
implemented query surface.

\paragraph{Where FHE still wins.}
Three regions remain genuine FHE-only territory:
(a)~encrypted-side conditionals whose branch depends on encrypted
predicates; (b)~non-polynomial functions of fiber values
(transcendentals, neural-network activations);
(c)~reductions over randomized ciphertexts whose intermediate
values must remain encrypted under noise --- order statistics on
PROBABILISTIC columns ($\sigma > 0$) are the canonical case, since
extreme values do not commute with additive Gaussian noise.
PROBABILISTIC's role is to trade exact order statistics for
statistical unlinkability under chosen-plaintext attack; the trade
is by design, not a limitation, and the implemented
order-statistic helpers refuse with a typed error on PROBABILISTIC columns rather
than silently return a biased value. Applications needing exact
extremes under noise either set the column to Affine ($\sigma = 0$)
or pair GIGI with FHE on the one column that needs it. The
recommended pattern is per-column FHE rather than wrapping the
entire database in FHE.

\paragraph{Confidentiality scope.}
The five encryption modes target different confidentiality
profiles, declared per-column at schema time. OPAQUE
(AES-256-GCM-SIV, RFC 8452) provides full IND-CPA confidentiality
on payload columns where no query surface is needed. INDEXED
(deterministic PRF) preserves $O(1)$ equality search on ciphertext
but leaks frequency by construction --- it is intended for
high-cardinality columns (UUIDs, row keys, 64-bit IDs);
low-cardinality categoricals must use OPAQUE or PROBABILISTIC.
PROBABILISTIC (Affine plus additive Gaussian noise) provides
bounded statistical unlinkability against chosen-plaintext
distinguishers (advantage scales with the schema-declared
$\sigma$) combined with approximate $O(1)$ bucket equality:
$\sigma$-resolution bucketing of the noised representation gives
a deterministic equivalence relation on ciphertexts that
approximates plaintext equality, with false-positive and
false-negative rates at the bucket boundaries that depend on
$\sigma$, the bucket width, and the field's value distribution
(see \S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}). Affine and
Isometric are deterministic and not IND-CPA secure on their own;
in deployments where an attacker may obtain
plaintext--ciphertext pairs, apply OPAQUE to those columns. Above
the per-column profile, Sprint O credential-gated invariant
queries authorize specific query classes at the API surface,
refusing ad-hoc callbacks not in the invariant ring.

The gauge modes ($\mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})$, Probabilistic,
$E(k)$) are property-preserving representations rather than
confidentiality primitives in their own right --- they do not
provide IND-CPA hiding without being paired with a confidentiality
primitive (typically OPAQUE on the sensitive columns), and the
``PQ?'' question therefore does not apply to them directly.
Seven of the eight cryptographic primitive classes are
post-quantum acceptable at NIST Level 1 or higher: the AES-256
family (OPAQUE + INDEXED), the SHA-256-based constructions
(Curvature-MAC, Holonomy Ledger, HKDF ratchet, \v{C}ech share
authentication), Shamir over the secp256k1 base field
(\v{C}ech threshold sharing --- we use the \emph{field}, not the

(FIPS 203). The exception is BLS12-381 pairing delegation
(Sprint J.2), whose $rk$-unforgeability rests on an
exponent-ratio hardness assumption in $G_2$ that is efficiently
solvable given discrete logarithms --- broken by Shor's
algorithm. Two PQ-safe
delegation paths are implemented alongside it: ML-KEM trusted-delegatee
(Sprint J.3, the PQ analogue of the BLS12-381 mode for single-party
recipients), and lattice threshold (Sprint J.4, Shamir $K$-of-$N$
composed with per-share ML-KEM transport, giving
information-theoretic collusion-resistance against any
$K{-}1$-shareholder coalition).

\paragraph{Delegation trust models.}
The four delegation modes (Aff$(\mathbb{R})$ algebraic,
BLS12-381 pairing, ML-KEM trusted, and lattice threshold) are
not substitutable; the right choice depends on the deployment
shape:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item For \textbf{$K$-of-$N$ distributed delegation} (Alice
  delegates to a quorum), the lattice threshold construction is
  the only implemented path that achieves true collusion-resistance:
  it is post-quantum and any subset of size $\leq K-1$ recovers
  \emph{information-theoretically zero} from the inner Shamir
  layer. Exhaustively verified for $(K{=}3, N{=}5)$ across all
  $\binom{5}{2} = 10$ collusion subsets in the public test suite.
\item For \textbf{single-party delegation Alice $\to$ Bob} (one
  designated delegatee, no quorum), the three implemented paths ---
  the Aff$(\mathbb{R})$ algebraic mode (Sprint J), the BLS12-381
  pairing mode (Sprint J.2), and the ML-KEM-768 trusted-delegatee
  mode (Sprint J.3) --- are all bearer-capability primitives: the
  delegation token is unforgeable without Alice's secret key (the
  hardness floor varies per mode), but once Bob holds the token
  any party Bob shares it with can derive the shared key. None of
  the three implemented single-party modes achieves
  delegatee-vs-proxy collusion-resistance in the
  Ateniese--Hohenberger sense. True single-party collusion-resistant
  PRE --- whether classical (AHP with capsule--pubkey binding) or
  post-quantum (lattice-PRE in the
  Kirshanova--Aono-Hayashi sense) --- is the deferred construction
  discussed in the companion preprint.
\end{itemize}

All four delegation modes are implemented; users select per
their trust model.

\subsection{Roadmap}
\label{sec:intro:roadmap}

\S\ref{sec:background} reviews the prior canon --- three Davis
manuscripts that this work extends --- plus the external math
anchors. \S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance} states and proves the
gauge-equivariance theorem: a query
$f: \Gamma(E) \to Y_f$ is computable on encrypted data with
closed-form client post-processing iff it is $\rho$-equivariant
under the structure group $G$ for some efficiently invertible
action $\rho_g: Y_f \to Y_f$. The invariant case
($\rho \equiv \mathrm{id}$) is the no-post-processing slice. The
companion preprint develops the five modes and the higher-level
constructions in turn, reports the empirical evaluation across
the implemented suites, and presents the three deferred constructions.

\subsection{Prior canon --- three Davis manuscripts}
\label{sec:background:canon}

This paper is the cryptographic application of three prior
Davis works --- two geometric substrates (the Davis Manifold and
the K\"ahler Substrate) plus the section-graph runtime
(Sheaf Composition) on which both substrates execute:

\paragraph{The Davis Manifold~\cite{davis2026manifold}.}
A Riemannian substrate $(M, g, P, \varepsilon, \kappa_{\text{hard}},
\kappa_{\text{soft}})$ for \emph{residue-bounded computation}:
$M$ is the manifold of admissible computational states, $g$ the
metric measuring how far two states diverge, $P$ a transport
operator, $\varepsilon$ the per-step error rate, and
$\kappa_{\text{hard}}, \kappa_{\text{soft}}$ a pair of error
budgets (hard = the absolute ceiling, soft = the regime in which
the computation is still considered consistent). The substrate's
load-bearing inequality, the \emph{non-vacuity condition}
$\kappa_{\text{soft}} - 2R\varepsilon(L^*) > 0$, certifies that
the soft budget exceeds twice the radius-times-Lipschitz error
product; when it holds, $M$ admits Hadamard regions in which any
two states are connected by a unique geodesic, and transport
under bounded noise remains invertible. The continuous RG-flow
ratchet of \S 9 advances the encryption gauge per write while
preserving this Hadamard certification --- fine-resolution
plaintext below the bin width is thermodynamically erased, but
the runtime stays in the regime where gauge-invariant queries are
still well-defined.

\paragraph{The K\"ahler Substrate~\cite{davis2026kahlersubstrate}.}
Extends $(M, g)$ to a K\"ahler manifold
$(M, g, J, \nabla, B, \Gamma)$: a complex structure $J$ satisfying
$J^2 = -I$, a Levi-Civita connection $\nabla$, a closed magnetic
2-form $B$ (the curvature of a line bundle over $M$, encoding
the geometry's holonomic content), and a discrete graph
approximation $\Gamma$ on which the continuous geometry is
evaluated in practice. The eight-layer catalog (L1--L8 plus
extensions) develops the gauge invariants this paper consumes:
scalar curvature $K$, spectral gap $\lambda_1$, Davis capacity
$C = \tau / K$, mean holonomy $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$, and the
Betti numbers $\beta_0, \beta_1$ of the field-index bitmap graph.
Together these form the six-component invariant fingerprint
$\pi_{\mathrm{inv}} = (K, \lambda_1, \overline{\mathrm{Hol}},
\tau, \beta_0, \beta_1)$ that the integrity tag of \S 5 signs
and the deterministic verifier of \S 10 recomputes from
ciphertext alone.

\paragraph{Sheaf Composition~\cite{davis2026sheafcomposition}.}
The discrete section-graph runtime that both substrates execute
on. Records are stored as sections of a fiber bundle, queries are
evaluated by composing operations on sections, and the encryption
gauge acts pointwise on each section's fiber values without
touching the base graph or the sheaf-glueing relations between
sections. This paper inherits that runtime verbatim: the
encryption modes of the companion preprint's \S 4 are gauge
actions on the same section-graph, and the integrity,
delegation, ledger, and verification constructions of the
companion preprint's \S\S 5--13 all compose against the same
fiber-bundle structure without modifying it.

\subsection{The fiber-bundle data model}
\label{sec:background:bundle}

A bundle $B = (E, B, F, \pi, \Phi)$ stores records as sections of the
bundle. The base space $B$ is parameterized by the GIGI hash
$G: K_1 \times \cdots \times K_m \to \mathbb{Z}_2^{64}$ of the record's
primary keys. The fiber $F = F_1 \times \cdots \times F_k$ is the
Cartesian product of non-key field types. Write $\Gamma(E)$ for the
space of sections of $E \xrightarrow{\pi} B$. A record is a section
$\sigma \in \Gamma(E)$ whose pointwise image satisfies
$\sigma(p) = (p, v_1, \ldots, v_k) \in E$ for each base point $p$.
We will use $\sigma \in \Gamma(E)$ throughout when we mean ``a
section'' and $e \in E$ when we mean a point in the total space; on
encrypted writes the encryption map $\mathrm{Enc}_g$ acts on the
section by transforming each pointwise fiber value, hence
$\mathrm{Enc}_g: \Gamma(E) \to \Gamma(E)$.

\paragraph{A worked example.}
Consider a three-record patient table with primary key
\texttt{patient\_id} and three fiber fields --- numeric
\texttt{age} and \texttt{pressure}, and categorical
\texttt{diagnosis}:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
\texttt{patient\_id} & \texttt{age} & \texttt{pressure} & \texttt{diagnosis} \\
\midrule
P001 & 47 & 130.5 & hypertension \\
P002 & 62 & 142.0 & hypertension \\
P003 & 35 & 118.3 & healthy \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The fiber-bundle representation is:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Base space.} $B = \{p_1, p_2, p_3\}$ with
  $p_i = G(\texttt{P00}i) \in \mathbb{Z}_2^{64}$; each base point is
  the keyed hash of the corresponding primary key.
\item \textbf{Fiber.} $F = \texttt{INT} \times \texttt{FLOAT}
  \times \texttt{TEXT}$ --- the Cartesian product of the three
  non-key field types.
\item \textbf{Sections.} Each row is a section
  $\sigma_i \in \Gamma(E)$:
  \[
  \begin{aligned}
  \sigma_1(p_1) &= (p_1,\, 47,\, 130.5,\, \text{`hypertension'}),\\
  \sigma_2(p_2) &= (p_2,\, 62,\, 142.0,\, \text{`hypertension'}),\\
  \sigma_3(p_3) &= (p_3,\, 35,\, 118.3,\, \text{`healthy'}).
  \end{aligned}
  \]
\end{itemize}
An Affine gauge $g = (a, b)$ applied to the \texttt{age} column
transforms $\sigma_1$ into
\[
\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma_1)(p_1) \;=\; \bigl(p_1,\; 47 a + b,\;
130.5,\; \text{`hypertension'}\bigr).
\]
The base point $p_1$ is unchanged, only the \texttt{age} fiber
value is transformed. An attacker observing only the encrypted
bundle sees the base hashes (which hide raw key values under
the PRF security of $G$ but still reveal equality and
access-pattern structure --- the standard deterministic-hash
leakage profile) and the encrypted fiber values
$\{47 a + b,\, 62 a + b,\, 35 a + b\}$. An analyst
without the gauge key but querying
\verb|PROJECT INVARIANT (curvature) ON age| computes
$K = \mathrm{Var}/\mathrm{range}^2$ directly on the encrypted
column and obtains the same number as on plaintext, because $K$
is gauge-invariant under $\mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})$ (the affine
scaling cancels between numerator and denominator;
Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}).

\begin{definition}[Dispersion ratio $K$]
\label{def:dispersion}
For a numeric fiber field $F_i$ with finite-sample variance
$\mathrm{Var}_i$ and range $\mathrm{range}_i = \max_i - \min_i > 0$,
define the per-field \emph{dispersion ratio}
\[
K_i \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{Var}_i}{\mathrm{range}_i^2}\,.
\]
The bundle-level dispersion is the unweighted mean over numeric
fields, $K = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}|}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{N}} K_i$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Naming]
We sometimes call $K$ the bundle's ``scalar curvature'' following the
Davis~Manifold~\cite{davis2026manifold} naming convention, where
the dispersion ratio is identified with a discrete scalar curvature on
the section-graph manifold of the Davis substrate. The identification
is not assumed in this paper. Where $K$ appears in any claim or proof
here, only the definition above is used.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Mean holonomy $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$]
\label{def:hol-mean}
Let $\Gamma_{\!B}$ be the discrete base graph of the bundle
(vertices = distinct base points, edges = field-index adjacencies
induced by the GIGI hash), and let $\beta_0, \beta_1$ be its $0$th
and $1$st Betti numbers (number of connected components; number
of independent cycles). The \emph{mean holonomy} is the
base-graph topological ratio
\[
\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}(B) \;=\; \frac{\beta_1}{\beta_0 + 1}.
\]
This is a base-only quantity: it depends purely on the base graph's
topology, not on fiber values. The motivation for naming it ``mean
holonomy'' is the proxy relationship to gauge-group holonomy:
non-trivial gauge holonomy is possible only when $\beta_1 > 0$
(cycles exist), and is increasingly likely as $\beta_1$ grows
relative to the number of components. The ratio is therefore an
upper-bound proxy for holonomic richness that is computable from
the base graph alone (no fiber decryption required).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Gauge-group holonomy as a separate primitive]
A separate query, the GQL-level \texttt{HOLONOMY \ldots ON FIBER}
statement, computes the discrete Gauss-Bonnet angle-deficit
holonomy in a $2$-fiber $(f_0, f_1)$-plane around a chosen base
field. That operation reads stored fiber values, but normalizes
centroids by their own min/range per axis before computing angles,
which makes the deficit gauge-invariant modulo $2\pi$ under any
per-field Aff$(\mathbb{R})$ gauge --- the normalization lives in
\texttt{src/bin/gigi\_stream.rs::compute\_fiber\_holonomy} and is
exercised by \texttt{tests/holonomy\_gauge\_invariance\_v0\_3.rs}
across a 20-gauge random sweep (including per-axis scale
mismatches, negative scales, and large translations). The
\texttt{HOLONOMY} statement therefore does not require decryption:
it can be evaluated on ciphertext and returns the same deficit
(mod $2\pi$) as it would on plaintext.

Throughout this paper, $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$ refers to the
base-graph topological ratio of
Definition~\ref{def:hol-mean} ($\beta_1 / (\beta_0 + 1)$), which is
the invariant-ring quantity reported by
\texttt{HolonomyAvg} in
\texttt{src/invariant.rs}~\cite{gigi2026code}. The \texttt{HOLONOMY
ON FIBER} GQL statement is a related but distinct primitive
(geometric Gauss-Bonnet on a fiber plane), not the
$\beta$-Betti-ratio that appears in the invariant tuple. The
detailed cases for $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Tree-like base ($\beta_1 = 0$): $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}} = 0$.
\item One connected component with $k$ cycles:
  $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}} = k/2$.
\item Multiple components, no cycles: $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}} = 0$.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Invariant tuple]
\label{def:invariant-tuple}
The bundle's six-component invariant fingerprint:
\[
\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}(B) \;=\;
\bigl( K,\; \lambda_1,\; \overline{\mathrm{Hol}},\; \tau,\; \beta_0,\; \beta_1 \bigr)
\]
where $K$ is the dispersion ratio (Definition~\ref{def:dispersion}),
$\lambda_1 := \lambda_1(L)$ is the smallest non-zero eigenvalue of
the combinatorial graph Laplacian $L = D - A$ on the field-index
bitmap graph $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$ (the spectral gap;
equivalently the algebraic connectivity à la Fiedler),
$\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$ is the mean holonomy
(Definition~\ref{def:hol-mean}), $\tau$ is the record count,
and $\beta_0, \beta_1$ are the $0$th and $1$st Betti numbers of
$\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$. This tuple is the load-bearing object
for Curvature-MAC (\S 5).
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Which graph carries which invariant.}
The graph-based invariants in $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ are distributed
across two related objects --- the field-index bitmap graph
$\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$ (vertices~=~distinct fiber-value
equivalence classes per indexed field, edges~=~field-index
adjacencies) and the discrete base graph $\Gamma_{\!B}$
(vertices~=~base points, edges induced by
$\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$ adjacencies on the records'
field-index entries). Under value-injective gauge transformations
the edge relations used to construct $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$
are preserved, and the induced base-graph topology is unchanged.
The per-invariant attribution below clarifies which graph each
component is computed on:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{p{2.3cm}p{4.0cm}p{6.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Quantity} & \textbf{Built on} & \textbf{Preserved under Aff$(\mathbb{R})$ because} \\
\midrule
$K_i$ (per-field) & $\mathrm{Var}_i / \mathrm{range}_i^2$ of fiber values in column $i$ & $a^2$ cancels between numerator and denominator (Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}) \\
\addlinespace
$\lambda_1$ & Laplacian of $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$ & affine injectivity preserves value-equivalence classes; same graph $\Rightarrow$ same Laplacian spectrum \\
\addlinespace
$\beta_0, \beta_1$ & Betti numbers of $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$ & same graph $\Rightarrow$ same Betti numbers \\
\addlinespace
$\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$ & $\beta_1(\Gamma_{\!B}) / (\beta_0(\Gamma_{\!B}) + 1)$ & base-graph topology unchanged (encryption acts on fibers only) \\
\addlinespace
$\tau$ & $|B|$ (record count) & base set cardinality unchanged \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Probabilistic and Opaque modes break the value-injectivity
condition (fresh randomness on each encryption), so their
preservation guarantees are weaker: $\lambda_1$ and the Betti
numbers are preserved only up to the equivalence relation the
mode actually preserves (bucket-equivalence for Probabilistic;
nothing on the fiber for Opaque). See \S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}
for the per-mode treatment.

\begin{remark}[What the Curvature-MAC tag does and does not protect]
\label{rmk:cmac-scope}
Curvature-MAC (\S 5) HMACs the canonical encoding of
$\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}(B)$ under a key derived from the gauge.
It detects edits to any of $(K, \lambda_1, \overline{\mathrm{Hol}},
\tau, \beta_0, \beta_1)$ exceeding the $10^{-10}$ quantization grain
--- i.e., \emph{any gauge-invariant content change}: record
insertion, deletion, or value change large enough to perturb a
component. It does \emph{not} on its own detect (a) ciphertext edits
that preserve all six invariants (a permutation of records, a
$+\varepsilon$ shift below the quantization grain) --- the
extended-leaf ledger of Sprint K (\S 7), which signs a per-record
\texttt{record\_hash}, closes this gap byte-level; (b) replay of an
old valid bundle under the same gauge --- the ledger's
append-only timestamp ordering closes this; (c) edits to
non-bundle data on the wire --- standard TLS / AEAD authentication
covers this layer. The pairing
(Curvature-MAC, ledger) is by design layered: invariant-level
attestation + per-record byte attestation, the way RFC 6962 layers
SCTs over per-certificate hashes.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Encryption modes and their mathematical structure}
\label{sec:background:groups}

\begin{definition}[Gauge action of an encryption mode]
\label{def:gauge-action}
An \emph{encryption mode} is a triple $(\mathcal{M}, \alpha, \mathcal{K})$
where $\mathcal{M}$ is a mathematical structure (a group, a quotient
map, or a randomized algorithm; see Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy} below),
$\alpha$ is the per-record action of $\mathcal{M}$ on a fiber tuple
$(v_1, \ldots, v_k) \in F$, and $\mathcal{K}$ is the key space
parameterizing $\mathcal{M}$. The base-space coordinates (keys) are
unchanged by $\alpha$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
For the two deterministic-bijective modes (Affine and Isometric),
$\mathcal{M}$ is a genuine structure group $G \subset \mathrm{Aut}(F)$
acting invertibly and affinely on $F$ (linearly after a
homogeneous-coordinate embedding that absorbs the translation). For the three non-deterministic-bijective
modes (Opaque, Indexed, Probabilistic), $\mathcal{M}$ is a wider
mathematical object: Opaque uses a randomized AEAD, so the same
plaintext maps to a probability distribution over ciphertexts;
Indexed uses a deterministic but one-way PRF, so the action is a
quotient map rather than a bijection; Probabilistic combines a
bijective Aff($\mathbb{R}$) gauge with additive Gaussian noise.
Throughout the paper, we use ``structure group'' interchangeably
with ``encryption structure'' for the deterministic-bijective modes,
and specify the mathematical object explicitly when the mode is not
a group action.
\end{remark}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{p{2cm}p{9cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Mode} & \textbf{Mathematical structure $\mathcal{M}$} \\
\midrule
Affine & Structure group $G = \mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})^k$ acting field-wise: $g_i(v) = a_i v + b_i$ with $a_i \neq 0$. \\
Isometric & Structure group $G = E(k) = O(k) \ltimes \mathbb{R}^k$ on a grouped $k$-tuple of numeric fields: $g(v) = O v + b$ with $O^\top O = I$; the orthogonal-only case $O(k)$ is recovered by setting $b = 0$. \\
Opaque & Randomized AEAD on the byte encoding of a fiber field. Same plaintext maps to a distribution over ciphertexts (per-record nonce). Not a deterministic gauge; not invertible without the AEAD key. \\
Indexed & Deterministic PRF $F_k: F \to \{0,1\}^n$ keyed by $k$. The induced equivalence relation $v_1 \sim v_2 \Leftrightarrow F_k(v_1) = F_k(v_2)$ is the structure preserved on ciphertext; the encryption is a quotient map, not a bijection. \\
Probabilistic & Aff($\mathbb{R}$) gauge $(a, b)$ plus i.i.d.~additive noise: $g(v) = a v + b + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. Bijective in expectation, randomized per-record. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Encryption-mode taxonomy. Affine and Isometric carry a true
structure group; Opaque is randomized; Indexed is a deterministic
quotient; Probabilistic combines an Aff group action with additive
Gaussian noise.}
\label{tab:taxonomy}
\end{table}

The fundamental claim of \S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance}: the query class
computable on encrypted data depends on which sub-structure of
$\mathcal{M}$ is preserved by the encryption. For group-action modes,
this reduces to the structure group's invariant theory; for
randomized or quotient-map modes, it requires a separate
characterization per mode (developed in \S\S 4--9 of the
companion preprint).

\section{The gauge-equivariance theorem}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance:setup}

\emph{Bundle, gauge, and query.}
Let $B = (E, B, F, \pi, \Phi)$ be a fiber-bundle data store with
structure group $G \subset \mathrm{Aut}(F)$, and let $\Gamma(E)$
denote the space of sections (the bundle's record set; §2.3). Let
$g \in G$ be a secret gauge transformation. Let
$\mathrm{Enc}_g: \Gamma(E) \to \Gamma(E)$ be the geometric encryption
under $g$:
\[
\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)(p) \;=\; \bigl(p,\, g(v_1, \ldots, v_k)\bigr)
\]
(base point $p$ unchanged; only fiber values transformed). Let
$f: \Gamma(E) \to \mathbb{R}$ be a query function on the bundle.

\begin{definition}[Gauge invariance]
\label{def:gauge-invariance}
$f$ is \emph{gauge-invariant under $G$} iff for every $g \in G$ and
every section $\sigma \in \Gamma(E)$:
\[
f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) \;=\; f(\sigma).
\]
\end{definition}

\subsection{The framework: equivariance and ciphertext computability}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance:thm}

\emph{From invariance to equivariance.}
The reviewer-friendly statement of the framework is not an
invariance characterization but an \emph{equivariance}
characterization. Many of the queries an analyst actually wants ---
$\textsc{sum}$, $\textsc{avg}$, $\textsc{min}$, $\textsc{max}$,
$\textsc{var}$, $\textsc{stddev}$, $\textsc{range}$ over affine
ciphertext --- are not gauge-invariant (the encrypted sum is
\emph{not} the plaintext sum). They are
\emph{$G$-equivariant} via a closed-form representation $\rho$, and
the client recovers the plaintext answer by applying $\rho_g^{-1}$
once at $O(1)$ cost. The invariant case is the special instance
$\rho \equiv \mathrm{id}$. We state the unified theorem first; the
invariant ring of \S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:ring} and the
client-inversion helpers of \S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}
are both corollaries.

\begin{definition}[Equivariant query]
\label{def:equivariant-query}
Let $G$ act on $\Gamma(E)$ via $\mathrm{Enc}_g$
(Definition~\ref{def:gauge-action}). Let $f: \Gamma(E) \to Y_f$
be a query function with answer set $Y_f$, and let
$\rho: G \to \mathrm{Sym}(Y_f)$ be an action of $G$ on $Y_f$ by
efficiently invertible maps --- i.e., for each $g \in G$ the map
$\rho_g: Y_f \to Y_f$ is a bijection whose inverse $\rho_g^{-1}$
is computable in time $O(\mathrm{poly}(|g|, |y|))$ in the
description sizes of the gauge and the answer. The map $\rho_g$
may additionally depend on bundle-derived public parameters that
are themselves computable on ciphertext (e.g., the record count
$\tau$ for $\textsc{sum}$); we write this dependency implicitly.

$f$ is \emph{$\rho$-equivariant under $G$} iff for every
$g \in G$ and every $\sigma \in \Gamma(E)$:
\[
f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) \;=\; \rho_g\bigl(f(\sigma)\bigr).
\]
When $\rho_g \equiv \mathrm{id}_{Y_f}$ for all $g$, $f$ is
\emph{gauge-invariant} (Definition~\ref{def:gauge-invariance},
the trivial-representation case).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Equivariant ciphertext-computability]
\label{thm:equivariance}
Let $G$ act on $\Gamma(E)$ via $\mathrm{Enc}_g$ with
$\mathrm{Enc}_e = \mathrm{id}_{\Gamma(E)}$, let $Y_f$ be a query
answer set, and let $\rho: G \to \mathrm{Sym}(Y_f)$ be an action
by efficiently invertible maps. For $f: \Gamma(E) \to Y_f$ the
following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),nosep]
\item \textbf{(Equivariance.)} $f$ is $\rho$-equivariant:
  $f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) = \rho_g(f(\sigma))$ for all
  $g, \sigma$.
\item \textbf{(Ciphertext-side computability up to $\rho$.)}
  There exists a deterministic algorithm
  $A_\rho: \Gamma(E) \to Y_f$ such that
  $A_\rho(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) = \rho_g(f(\sigma))$ for all
  $g \in G, \sigma \in \Gamma(E)$.
\item \textbf{(Client-side recovery with closed-form
  post-processing.)} There exists $A_\rho$ as in (b) such that
  the client, given the secret gauge $g$, recovers the plaintext
  answer via
  \[
  f(\sigma) \;=\; \rho_g^{-1}\!\bigl(A_\rho(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma))\bigr),
  \]
  at cost $O(\mathrm{poly}(|g|, |y|))$ \emph{independent of $|E|$}.
\end{enumerate}
The invariant special case $\rho \equiv \mathrm{id}$ collapses
(b)/(c) to $A(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) = f(\sigma)$ --- the
no-post-processing regime --- and is recovered as
Corollary~\ref{cor:invariant-special-case}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{(a) $\Rightarrow$ (b).} Take $A_\rho = f$. For any $\sigma, g$:
$A_\rho(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) = f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma))
= \rho_g(f(\sigma))$ by (a). So $A_\rho = f$ witnesses (b).

\emph{(b) $\Rightarrow$ (c).} By hypothesis $\rho_g: Y_f \to Y_f$
is invertible with $\rho_g^{-1}$ computable in
$O(\mathrm{poly}(|g|, |y|))$; the client computes $\rho_g^{-1}$
from $g$ and applies it to the server's output. The cost is in
the description sizes of $g$ and the answer, independent of $|E|$.

\emph{(c) $\Rightarrow$ (a).} Let $A_\rho$ witness (c). Fix any
$\sigma$ and $g$, and let $\sigma' := \mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)$. Apply
$A_\rho$ to $\sigma'$ along two paths:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Path 1 (true gauge $g$): $\sigma' = \mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)$, so
  by (c): $A_\rho(\sigma') = \rho_g(f(\sigma))$.
\item Path 2 (identity gauge $e$): $\sigma' = \mathrm{Enc}_e(\sigma')$
  since $\mathrm{Enc}_e = \mathrm{id}_{\Gamma(E)}$, and by (c)
  applied to $\sigma'$ and gauge $e$:
  $A_\rho(\sigma') = \rho_e(f(\sigma')) = f(\sigma')$ because
  $\rho$ is a homomorphism so $\rho_e = \mathrm{id}_{Y_f}$.
\end{itemize}
Equating: $\rho_g(f(\sigma)) = f(\sigma')
= f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma))$. Since $g, \sigma$ were arbitrary,
$f$ is $\rho$-equivariant.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Invariant special case]
\label{cor:invariant-special-case}
With $\rho \equiv \mathrm{id}$,
Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance} reduces to: $f$ is $G$-invariant
iff there exists $A: \Gamma(E) \to Y_f$ with
$A(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) = f(\sigma)$ for all $g, \sigma$. This
recovers the ``invariance characterizes ciphertext computability''
slice as a corollary of the equivariant theorem.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Why equivariance is the right framework]
Stating the result as pure ``invariance characterizes ciphertext
computability'' would systematically undercount the
ciphertext-computable query class. $\textsc{sum}$ on an
affine-encrypted column has answer space
$Y_{\textsc{sum}} = \mathbb{R}$ and gauge action
$\rho_g(s) = a \cdot s + n \cdot b$ parameterized by the
bundle-derived public count $n$ (which the server can also
compute, since $\textsc{count}$ is itself gauge-invariant). The
action is non-trivial, so $\textsc{sum}$ is \emph{not} invariant
but \emph{is} ciphertext-computable: the encrypted sum,
post-processed by $\rho_g^{-1}(\hat s) = (\hat s - n b)/a$ at the
client, equals the plaintext sum exactly. Analogous answer
spaces and actions recover AVG ($Y = \mathbb{R}$,
$\rho_g(\mu) = a\mu + b$), VAR/STDDEV/RANGE (scalar actions in
$a$ alone), MEDIAN and QUANTILE (sign-aware quantile-flip on
$\mathbb{R}$), and ARGMIN/ARGMAX
($Y = [\tau] \times [\tau]$, $\rho_g$ a joint index swap when
$a < 0$) --- see \S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}.
Calling these queries ``not computable from the ciphertext''
would have been a strict factual error; the equivariance lens
repairs it.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Closed-form vs.\ general decryption]
The post-processing in (c) is $\rho_g^{-1}$, a closed-form
algebraic operation derived from $g$ and the query's
representation $\rho$. It is \emph{not} full decryption of the
bundle, and it is \emph{not} computation on plaintext; for the
analytical-SQL aggregates of \S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}
the client applies a single scalar division (or, for the joint
sign-aware aggregates ARGMIN/ARGMAX, a pair swap). The server's
compute remains over ciphertext at native speed, on a fully
encrypted bundle.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Equivariant ring vs.\ invariant ring]
For each structure group $G$ and answer-space action $\rho$ on
$Y_f$, Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance} identifies the
\emph{$\rho$-equivariant subspace}
$\mathcal{E}_{G,\rho} \subset \mathrm{Map}(\Gamma(E), Y_f)$. The
invariant ring $\mathcal{I}_G = \mathcal{E}_{G,\mathrm{id}}$ of
\S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:ring} is the trivial-representation
slice. The non-trivial slices give the analytical SQL surface
(SUM/AVG/etc.) developed in \S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}.
This is the database-runtime analogue of equivariant
representation theory: the ciphertext-computable algebra
\emph{decomposes by irreducible representation of $G$} into a
direct sum of equivariant subspaces, exactly one of which
(the trivial summand) is the invariant ring.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Affine-mode invariant ring contains the bundle's invariant tuple]
\label{thm:affine-invariants}
Let $G = \mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})^k$ act field-wise on the fiber
$F = \mathbb{R}^k$. The six components of $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}(B)$
(Definition~\ref{def:invariant-tuple}) lie in
$\mathcal{I}_G$, i.e.~each is fixed under any per-field affine
re-encryption with $a_i \neq 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We check each component:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $K = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}|}\sum_i \mathrm{Var}_i / \mathrm{range}_i^2$.
  Under $g_i(v) = a_i v + b_i$: $\mathrm{Var}_i(g_i(v)) = a_i^2 \mathrm{Var}_i(v)$,
  $\mathrm{range}_i(g_i(v)) = |a_i| \cdot \mathrm{range}_i(v)$, so the
  ratio is invariant. The mean over fields is invariant.
\item $\lambda_1$: the spectral gap is computed from the field-index
  bitmap topology (Definition~\ref{def:invariant-tuple}), which is a
  binary indicator of which records share which values. Affine
  per-field encryption is injective on each field, so the
  value-equivalence classes are preserved, hence the bitmap topology
  is preserved, hence $\lambda_1$ is bit-identical.
\item $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}} = \beta_1 / (\beta_0 + 1)$
  (Definition~\ref{def:hol-mean}) depends purely on the base graph's
  Betti numbers, which are preserved under affine per-field encryption
  (as in the $\lambda_1$ case). $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$ is therefore
  preserved bit-identically.
\item $\tau$: record count is unchanged by any deterministic
  encryption.
\item $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$: graph-topology invariants on the
  field-index bitmap; preserved under value-injective gauges as above.
\end{itemize}
All six components are therefore fixed under $G$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[The diagonal-affine group is the maximal affine linear gauge preserving the per-field $K$-vector universally]
\label{prop:k-preserving}
Let $F = \mathbb{R}^k$ with labeled coordinates and let
$K_{\mathrm{vec}}(X) = (K_1(X), \ldots, K_k(X))$ be the per-field
dispersion vector on a record set $X \subset F$ with
$K_i = \mathrm{Var}_i / \mathrm{range}_i^2$. Within the class of
affine linear gauges $g(v) = Av + b$ with $A \in
\mathrm{GL}_k(\mathbb{R})$, the largest subgroup that preserves
$K_{\mathrm{vec}}$ for \emph{all} nondegenerate record sets
$X \subset \mathbb{R}^k$ is the diagonal affine group
\[
G_{\mathrm{AffK}} \;=\; (\mathbb{R}^*)^k \ltimes \mathbb{R}^k
\;=\; \{\,g(v) = D v + b \mid D = \mathrm{diag}(a_1, \ldots, a_k),\ a_i \neq 0\,\}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\emph{Sufficiency} is Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}: the
diagonal $A = D$ scales $\mathrm{Var}_i$ and $\mathrm{range}_i^2$
by the same factor $a_i^2$, so $K_i$ is unchanged for every $X$.

\emph{Necessity.} Suppose $A$ is not diagonal. Then there exist
indices $i \neq j$ with $A_{ij} \neq 0$, so the post-gauge $i$-th
coordinate is $A_{ii} v_i + A_{ij} v_j + b_i$. The post-gauge
$\mathrm{Var}_i$ depends on $\mathrm{Var}_i, \mathrm{Var}_j$,
and $\mathrm{Cov}(v_i, v_j)$; a nondegenerate $X$ with
non-trivial $\mathrm{Cov}(v_i, v_j)$ produces a post-gauge
$K_i$ that differs from the plaintext $K_i$, witnessing
non-preservation. Pure rotations $A = O \in O(k)$ are the
canonical case: $O$ preserves $\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Cov})$ but
mixes the per-field ranges, so the per-field ratio breaks even
though the trace is invariant. Translations $b$ shift the mean
but leave $\mathrm{Var}_i$ and $\mathrm{range}_i$ unchanged, so
the translation part is free. The maximal universally-preserving
subgroup is therefore diagonal scalings plus translations,
i.e.\ $(\mathbb{R}^*)^k \ltimes \mathbb{R}^k$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The proposition is stated within the affine linear class and
asserts universality over nondegenerate $X$; it does not claim
maximality among nonlinear or measurable transformations, nor
does it preclude that for a specific degenerate $X$ a
non-diagonal $A$ may happen to preserve $K_{\mathrm{vec}}$. The
trace-form $K_{\mathrm{group}} = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Cov}) /
\mathrm{diam}^2$ on a grouped tuple
(\S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}, Isometric paragraph)
is the $E(k)$-invariant alternative that admits the full
Euclidean group, at the cost of conflating the per-field
structure into a single scalar.

\begin{remark}[On generating sets]
Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants} states containment in
$\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Aff}}$, not generation. The question of
whether these six scalars generate the full invariant ring is
mode-dependent and outside this paper's scope; we make claims only
about containment and about the gauge-rotation invariance of
$\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ under the listed modes.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Examples per mode}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}

\paragraph{Affine ($G = \mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})^k$).}
\emph{Invariant slice} ($\rho_g = \mathrm{id}$): the six
components of $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}); plus order and equality
predicates --- the latter via the affine path
$\textsc{where}\ v > c \mapsto \textsc{where}\ g(v) > g(c)$
(transform the literal, not the data; order is preserved iff $a > 0$,
equality is always bijective).
\smallskip

\noindent
\emph{Equivariant slice} (non-trivial $\rho_g$): the eleven
non-trivial analytical-SQL aggregates SUM, AVG, MIN, MAX, VAR,
STDDEV, RANGE, MEDIAN, QUANTILE, ARGMIN, ARGMAX (plus the
trivially gauge-invariant COUNT, for twelve operators total) are
computable on ciphertext at native server speed, then
inverted at the client in $O(1)$ via $\rho_g^{-1}$. For a single
column under $g(v) = a v + b$ over $n$ records:
\[
\begin{aligned}
&\rho_{\textsc{sum}}(s) = a s + n b,
\quad
&\rho_{\textsc{avg}}(\mu) = a \mu + b,\\
&\rho_{\textsc{min}}(m) = a m + b \;(a>0),
\quad
&\rho_{\textsc{max}}(M) = a M + b \;(a>0),\\
&\rho_{\textsc{var}}(V) = a^2 V,
\quad
&\rho_{\textsc{stddev}}(s) = |a| s,\\
&\rho_{\textsc{range}}(R) = |a| R,
\quad
&\rho_{\textsc{median}}(m) = a m + b,\\
&\rho_{\textsc{quantile}_q}(q_v) = a q_v + b \;(a>0),
\quad
&\rho_{\textsc{argmin/argmax}} = \text{joint sign-aware swap}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Each $\rho_g$ is a closed-form efficiently invertible map on
the appropriate answer space $Y_f$: for the scalar aggregates
(SUM, AVG, MIN, MAX, VAR, STDDEV, RANGE, MEDIAN, QUANTILE) the
inversion $\rho_g^{-1}$ is one division and one subtraction on
$\mathbb{R}$; for the sign-sensitive joint aggregates
ARGMIN/ARGMAX the inversion is a joint swap of the returned
index pair when $a < 0$ on $Y = [\tau] \times [\tau]$; and for
QUANTILE the analogous $q \mapsto 1 - q$ flip is applied at the
query layer when $a < 0$.
The reference implementation is
\texttt{src/aggregate\_helpers.rs} ($24$ unit tests: $16$ exactness
+ $8$ bias-refusal under Probabilistic-mode noise).

\emph{Concretely, SUM.}
Alice holds the gauge $g = (a, b)$ and ships an encrypted column
of $n$ records to Bob, the server. Bob evaluates
$\hat{s} = \sum_{i=1}^n g(v_i)$ over the ciphertext and returns
the single scalar $\hat{s} = a s + n b$, where
$s = \sum_i v_i$ is the plaintext sum. Alice recovers
$s = (\hat{s} - n b)/a$ in one subtraction and one division;
her work is $O(1)$ independent of $n$, and Bob's server-side
computation has run end-to-end on ciphertext. The remaining
aggregate operators follow the same pattern with their
respective $\rho_g^{-1}$ on the answer space appropriate to
each query.

\paragraph{Opaque (randomized AEAD).}
Functions of the encrypted fiber values are not invariant
(only constants), because AEAD output is a randomized function
of plaintext + nonce. Functions of \emph{the base alone} ---
record count $\tau$, the base-graph Betti numbers
$\beta_0(\Gamma_{\!B}), \beta_1(\Gamma_{\!B})$, and the schema
structure --- are trivially invariant because the encryption
does not touch the base space (Definition~\ref{def:gauge-action}).
The field-index bitmap graph $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fi}}$ and any
invariants computed on it are \emph{not} preserved under Opaque,
because randomized AEAD breaks the value-equivalence classes the
graph is built from. Computability class on the fiber: nothing
without decryption.

\paragraph{Indexed (deterministic PRF).}
$v_1 = v_2 \Rightarrow \mathrm{PRF}_k(v_1) = \mathrm{PRF}_k(v_2)$
deterministically; for distinct plaintexts, spurious-collision
probability is birthday-bounded by $O(q^2 / 2^{128})$ for $q$
indexed values under the AES-CMAC instantiation behaving as a
PRF (so spurious collisions on the encrypted side are negligible
for any practical $q$ and the verifier treats them as honest
content edits). Computability: equality
and equality-based queries (\textsc{group by}, \textsc{join} on
equality, bitmap-index lookup). Functions of the base also remain
trivially invariant. The structure group is degenerate (a
one-way quotient, not a group action), so
Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance} applies in the form ``the
equivariance class is the equality predicate $v_1 = v_2$,
realized through the PRF rather than through a closed-form
$\rho_g$.''

\begin{remark}[Equality predicate structure]
\label{rmk:indexed-eq}
In the implemented Indexed equality path, the final equality
predicate reduces to a fixed-width integer comparison over the
stored index tag; the source contains no data-dependent byte
loop for equality testing. This does not remove access-pattern
leakage from the index lookup (a HashMap probe still touches
data-dependent memory), but it avoids short-circuit timing
behavior in the equality comparison itself. This is structurally
different from byte-loop constant-time equality disciplines such
as \texttt{subtle::ConstantTimeEq}, which compare byte sequences
without data-dependent branches \emph{inside} the loop; here, no
loop body exists for a compiler or speculative-execution path to
short-circuit.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Probabilistic ($g(v) = av + b + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ per encryption).}
Probabilistic mode breaks exact equivariance: the per-encryption
noise $\varepsilon$ is fresh on every ciphertext, so
$\mathrm{Enc}_g$ is not deterministic and no $\rho_g$ literally
satisfies
$f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma)) = \rho_g(f(\sigma))$ pointwise.
Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance} therefore does not apply
directly --- it is replaced by an \emph{$\epsilon$-equivariance}
characterization in the concentration sense:
\[
\Pr\Bigl[\,\bigl|\,f(\mathrm{Enc}_g(\sigma))
- \rho_g(f(\sigma))\,\bigr| \;\geq\; \epsilon\,\Bigr]
\;\leq\; \delta(\epsilon, n, \sigma),
\]
where $n$ is the records-in-aggregate count and
$\delta(\epsilon, n, \sigma)$ is a concentration tail. For the
linear aggregates SUM / AVG with $g(v) = av + b$ and $n$
independent Gaussian noise draws:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{SUM.}\quad
  $\widehat{S}_n - (a S_n + n b) = \sum_{i=1}^n \varepsilon_i \sim
  \mathcal{N}(0, n \sigma^2)$ is exactly Gaussian, so the standard
  Gaussian tail
  \[
  \Pr\!\left[\,\bigl|\widehat{S}_n - (aS_n + nb)\bigr| \geq \epsilon\,\right]
  \;\leq\; 2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{\epsilon^2}{2 n \sigma^2}\right)
  \]
  holds without any boundedness assumption on the plaintexts. For
  $\epsilon = c \sigma \sqrt{n}$ the failure probability is
  $\leq 2 e^{-c^2/2}$.
\item \textbf{AVG.}\quad The mean error
  $\widehat{\mu}_n - (a \mu_n + b) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2/n)$
  is again exactly Gaussian and contracts at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$:
  $\Pr[|\widehat{\mu}_n - (a\mu_n + b)| \geq \epsilon]
  \leq 2 \exp(-n \epsilon^2 / (2\sigma^2))$.
\item \textbf{VAR.}\quad The estimator
  $\widehat{V}_n$ is a chi-square--scaled noisy variance whose
  sub-exponential tails require an a-priori bound on the
  plaintexts. \emph{Under the bounded-plaintext assumption}
  $|av + b| \leq M$ for all records, Bernstein's inequality yields
  the one-sided tail
  \[
  \Pr\!\left[\widehat{V}_n - (a^2 V_n + \sigma^2) \geq \epsilon\right]
  \leq \exp\!\bigl(-n\epsilon^2 / (2(2\sigma^4 + 2\sigma^2 M^2 + M \epsilon/3))\bigr).
  \]
  Without the bounded-plaintext assumption, the analogous
  sub-exponential concentration holds with constants depending on
  the moment bounds (see companion preprint).
\end{itemize}
The result is an $(\epsilon,\delta)$-equivariant ring under $\rho$
covering \emph{linear and quadratic aggregates}
(COUNT, SUM, AVG, VAR, STDDEV) with closed-form correction
$\rho_g^{-1}$ on the noisy estimator: the
client recovers the plaintext aggregate to within an additive
$O(\sigma/\sqrt{n})$ error with high probability.
\emph{Order statistics (MIN, MAX, RANGE, MEDIAN, QUANTILE,
ARGMIN, ARGMAX) are excluded under Probabilistic noise}: the
extreme-value and rank functionals do not commute with additive
Gaussian noise, and the naïve closed-form $\rho_g^{-1}$ overshoots
in expectation by $\Theta(\sigma\sqrt{2\log n}/|a|)$ for the
extremes (with analogous rank-bias terms for MEDIAN/QUANTILE/
ARGMIN/ARGMAX), a bias that does \emph{not} contract as
$n \to \infty$. \S\,\ref{sec:intro:not} documents the resulting
deployment caveat and the operational alternatives (switch the
column to Affine $\sigma = 0$ for plaintext-exact extremes; pair
GIGI with FHE on the specific column when exact extremes under
noise are required); the implemented helpers refuse the seven
order-statistic operators under $\sigma > 0$ with a typed
bias-refusal error so the bias cannot leak into application code
unnoticed.
\smallskip

\noindent
\emph{Approximate bucket equality via the Davis Identity.}
For normalized similarity $S$ and normalized squared distance
$d^2$, the \emph{Davis Identity} $S + d^2 = 1$ converts a
sameness scoring problem into a distance-threshold predicate:
two values are deemed equal iff their squared distance falls
below a chosen threshold. Applied to Probabilistic mode, this
yields a deterministic bucket relation on the noised
representation,
\[
v_1 \approx_\sigma v_2 \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\;
\mathrm{bucket}_\sigma(v_1) = \mathrm{bucket}_\sigma(v_2),
\]
and a corresponding approximate plaintext-equality predicate
whose false-positive and false-negative rates are controlled by
the noise scale, the bucket width $w$, and the boundary distance
of the test values.

\begin{proposition}[FP/FN tail bounds for $\sigma$-bucket equality]
\label{prop:bucket-fp-fn}
Let $w$ be the bucket width, let $\eta_1, \eta_2 \sim
\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ be the independent noise draws on the
two ciphertexts under test, and let $\Phi$ denote the standard
normal CDF.

\emph{False negatives (equal plaintexts split across a boundary).}
If a plaintext value lies at distance $\delta > 0$ from the
nearest bucket boundary, two independent noisy encryptions of
that same plaintext land on opposite sides of the boundary only
if at least one of $\eta_1, \eta_2$ crosses the boundary.
A union bound on the two single-noise crossing events yields
\[
\Pr[\text{FN at boundary distance }\delta]
\;\leq\;
2\,\Phi(-\delta / \sigma).
\]

\emph{False positives (distinct plaintexts colliding in one bucket).}
For two plaintexts separated by $\Delta$, the noisy separation
is $\Delta + Z$ with $Z = \eta_1 - \eta_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,
2\sigma^2)$. Bucket collision then requires $|\Delta + Z|$ to
fall below the bucket threshold relative to the nearest boundary,
and the false-positive rate is bounded by the corresponding
Gaussian tail on $Z$ at scale $\sqrt{2}\sigma$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent
The engineering practice is to choose $\sigma$ small relative to
the field's resolvable granularity and to align bucket boundaries
away from the most-probable test points so that boundary
collisions are rare in the target workload.

\paragraph{Isometric ($G = E(k) = O(k) \ltimes \mathbb{R}^k$ on a grouped $k$-tuple).}
We take the full Euclidean group $E(k) = O(k) \ltimes \mathbb{R}^k$
(rotations / reflections composed with translations) as the
structure group on a grouped $k$-tuple; the orthogonal-only case
$G = O(k)$ is the translation-fixed subgroup and is recovered by
setting the translation to $0$. Pairwise Euclidean distance
$\|u - v\|$ is preserved exactly under $E(k)$ --- both rotation
and translation preserve distances --- hence any distance-based
query (k-NN, clustering, distance threshold) is invariant.

Note that the dispersion ratio $K_i$
(Definition~\ref{def:dispersion}) is \emph{per-field} and is in
general \emph{not} preserved by $E(k)$ --- a rotation in $O(2)$ on
fields $(x, y)$ mixes the per-field variances and ranges, and a
translation shifts the per-field mins/maxes. The
$E(k)$-invariant generalization of dispersion is the
trace-form / diameter quotient
\[
K_{\mathrm{group}}(X) \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{tr}\,\mathrm{Cov}(X)}
                              {\mathrm{diam}^2(X)},
\qquad
\mathrm{diam}^2(X) \;:=\; \max_{p,q \in X}\, \|p - q\|^2,
\]
where $X = \{v^{(1)}, \ldots, v^{(n)}\} \subset \mathbb{R}^k$ is
the set of record-valued grouped vectors. Both numerator and
denominator are $E(k)$-invariant: the trace of the covariance
matrix is the sum of eigenvalues, which is conjugation-invariant
under $O(k)$ and unchanged by mean-shifts (translations); the
squared diameter is a max over pairwise Euclidean distances and is
preserved exactly under $E(k)$ by definition. The per-coordinate
range $(\max_i v - \min_i v)^2$ is \emph{not} $O(k)$-invariant
(the coordinate-wise extrema change under rotation), so the
squared-diameter denominator $\mathrm{diam}^2(X)$ above is the
correct $E(k)$-invariant choice.

The Curvature-MAC construction
(\S\,5) uses the per-field $K$ for fields
\emph{outside} an isometric group and $K_{\mathrm{group}}$ for
the group's grouped components; see \S\,5
for the precise mode-conditional definition. Mean holonomy
$\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:hol-mean}) depends
on the field-index topology rather than the value coordinates and
is preserved by $E(k)$ (the topology is unaffected by isometric
re-coordinatization of values).

\paragraph{Per-mode leakage profile.}
The honest summary of \emph{what each mode reveals to a
ciphertext-only adversary} is the table below. Leakage is graded by
the standard structured-encryption taxonomy of
Chase--Kamara~\cite{chase2010structured}: \emph{access pattern}
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{acc}}$ (which records were touched by which
query), \emph{equality pattern} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eq}}$ (which
pairs of records share a value on a queried field), \emph{order
pattern} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ord}}$ (the value-ordering on a
queried field), and \emph{distance pattern}
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{dist}}$ (pairwise Euclidean distances within
an isometrically-grouped tuple).
\begin{center}\footnotesize
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{|l|c|c|c|c|X|}
\hline
\textbf{Mode} & \textbf{Eq.} & \textbf{Ord.} & \textbf{Dist.} & \textbf{Aggregate} & \textbf{Adversary class} \\
\hline
Opaque (AEAD)              & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & IND-CPA / IND-CCA \\
Indexed (det.\ PRF)        & $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eq}}$ & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & PRF-secure, birthday-bounded $O(q^2/2^{128})$ \\
Affine (Aff$(\mathbb{R})$) & $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eq}}$ & $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ord}}$ & $\bot$ & \textbf{equiv.}~via $\rho_g$ & IND-OCPA (single column) \\
Probabilistic              & $\approx_\sigma$ & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & $(\epsilon,\delta)$-equiv.~via $\rho_g$ & noise-bucket OCPA ($\sigma$) \\
Isometric ($E(k)$)         & $\bot$ & $\bot$ & $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{dist}}$ & dist-invariant & distance-only (no per-coord) \\
\hline
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}
Three things to read out of this table:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Each mode's leakage profile is the \emph{minimal} information
  the gauge-equivariance structure forces the server to learn.
  Anything beyond this is a leakage bug, not a leakage feature.
\item \textbf{Equivariant computability is a separate axis from
  leakage}: the Affine row computes all twelve analytical-SQL
  aggregates (COUNT, SUM, AVG, MIN, MAX, VAR, STDDEV, RANGE,
  MEDIAN, QUANTILE, ARGMIN, ARGMAX) via the equivariant
  representation
  $\rho_g$ \emph{without expanding the access / order / equality
  leakage profile}; the Probabilistic row computes the linear and
  quadratic subset (COUNT, SUM, AVG, VAR, STDDEV) on the same axis
  with $(\epsilon, \delta)$-equivariance. The client's
  $\rho_g^{-1}$ is purely post-processing of the server's aggregate
  output.
\item All five modes share access-pattern leakage
  $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{acc}}$ at the bundle layer (which records
  are read by which query); the standard
  oblivious-RAM / private information retrieval mitigations apply
  uniformly and are orthogonal to the gauge-invariance
  construction.
\end{itemize}

The companion preprint develops the per-mode security proofs
(reductions and assumptions for IND-CPA, PRF security, the
exponent-ratio bearer-capability hardness assumption underlying
BLS12-381 delegation, and MLWE for the PQ paths) against the
leakage
profiles above; the present subsection's purpose is to make the
profile explicit so the framework theorem can be read against it.

\subsection{The invariant ring}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance:ring}

For each deterministic-bijective mode with structure group $G$, the
$G$-invariant ring is the subalgebra of real-valued bundle functions
fixed by $G$:
\[
\mathcal{I}_G \;=\; \bigl\{\, f \in \mathrm{Map}(\Gamma(E), \mathbb{R})
                          \;\big|\; f \circ \mathrm{Enc}_g = f
                          \text{ for all } g \in G \,\bigr\}.
\]
$\mathcal{I}_G$ is closed under pointwise $+$, $\times$, and
multiplication by scalars in $\mathbb{R}$. Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}
shows that for $G = \mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})^k$,
$\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Aff}}$ contains the six components of
$\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:invariant-tuple}). Further
$\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Aff}}$ contains every polynomial combination of
those components; whether they generate the full invariant ring is
left open here (see Remark on generating sets following
Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}). The
\verb|PROJECT INVARIANT(...)| query language is the parser surface
that admits polynomial-combination expressions over $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$;
it is by construction a sublanguage of $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Aff}}$.

\paragraph{Parse-time enforcement.}
The production \verb|PROJECT INVARIANT| grammar accepts only an
AST generated by invariant-ring constructors. Non-invariant
operators such as \verb|sum|, \verb|mean|, or raw field projection
fail at parse time, before query planning or execution: the
decryption code path is unreachable from any query that compiles
through the \verb|PROJECT INVARIANT| surface. The runtime
gauge-falsification harness (\S\ref{sec:intro:adds}, item 3) is a
test/development guard for ad-hoc query callbacks supplied outside
the certified grammar; the parser is the load-bearing enforcement
mechanism. This pattern --- restricting the query grammar to admit
only operations on a committed invariant subspace --- is the
database-runtime analogue of the privacy-preserving-blueprints
proof discipline~\cite{kohlweiss2023blueprints}, in which the
admissible statements about a committed value are restricted to
the policy-satisfying subspace.

\subsection{Floating-point reality}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance:fp}

Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance} (and its invariant
specialization Corollary~\ref{cor:invariant-special-case}) hold in
exact arithmetic. In floating-point implementation, gauge
invariance/equivariance holds only \emph{up to} the precision of
the arithmetic. Specifically, re-encrypting a
bundle under a new gauge $g'$ (the operation Sprint G's
\verb|ROTATE_KEY| performs) applies one affine round-trip
$v \mapsto g(v) \mapsto g^{-1}(g(v)) \mapsto g'(g^{-1}(g(v)))$ to each
field, introducing $\sim 10^{-13}$ relative error per traversal.

The effect on the components of the invariant tuple
$\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}(B)$ (Definition~\ref{def:invariant-tuple}):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, $\tau$ are integers (Betti numbers and
  record count) --- exact, zero drift.
\item $\lambda_1$ is a deterministic graph invariant of the
  $0$/$1$ field-index bitmap topology --- preserved \emph{as a
  mathematical object} under any value-preserving gauge
  transformation, because the underlying graph is unchanged. The
  implementation stores a canonical quantized numerical
  approximation (see Quantization grain below); invariance holds
  because the graph is the same, not because the floating-point
  eigenvalue is itself an integer.
\item $K = \mathrm{Var}/\mathrm{range}^2$ drifts by $\sim 10^{-11}$
  relative through the Welford-streaming variance computation across
  the rotated record set --- the only f64 component with any drift.
\item $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}} = \beta_1 / (\beta_0 + 1)$ is a ratio
  of integer Betti numbers and is therefore \emph{exact}: zero
  drift under any gauge that preserves the base-graph topology.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why $\tau$ enters $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ directly.}
The Davis capacity $C = \tau / K$ is fully recoverable on demand
from $K$ and $\tau$; storing $C$ in the integrity-tag input adds
no information beyond what the tag already binds, and would
amplify $K$'s relative drift by a factor of $\tau$ through the
division (e.g.\ a $40$-record bundle would push the drift on $C$
into the $\sim 10^{-9}$ regime). $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ therefore
carries the exact $u64$ record-count $\tau$ directly; any caller
needing capacity recomputes $\tau / K$ trivially after retrieving
the tuple.

\paragraph{Quantization grain.}
With $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$ a Betti-ratio (integer-exact) and
$\lambda_1$ a canonical-quantized graph invariant of the $0$/$1$
bitmap (deterministic across runs on the same graph topology),
the only f64 component with any data-dependent drift is $K$. We
quantize the f64 slots ($K$, $\lambda_1$, $\overline{\mathrm{Hol}}$)
uniformly to $10$ decimal digits before HMAC:
\[
\mathrm{quantize}(x) \;=\; \mathrm{round}(x \cdot 10^{10}) \in \mathbb{Z}.
\]
The measured worst-case drift on any f64 component is now $\sim 10^{-13}$
absolute (on $K$), $3$ orders of magnitude below the $10^{-10}$
quantization grain. The framework's gauge-invariance
property (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance} +
Corollary~\ref{cor:invariant-special-case} +
Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}) therefore holds bit-identically
through the integrity tag in practice. The quantization grain is
chosen $3$ orders of magnitude looser than the measured drift so
that legitimate floating-point noise rounds away \emph{below} the
HMAC input; any genuine drift in the invariants reaching the
$10^{-10}$ digit is preserved through the tag and detected as a
content edit by the verifier.

\subsection{Roadmap to the companion preprint}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance:implications}

Internal references to \S\S 4--18 throughout this manuscript point
to sections of the companion preprint, which writes up the ten
cryptographic constructions and the empirical evaluation that ride
on top of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance}:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \S 4 --- the five-mode taxonomy with mathematical structure
  per mode.
\item \S 5 --- Curvature-MAC: an HMAC on the invariant tuple,
  gauge-rotation-invariant in floating-point thanks to the $10$-dp
  quantization. (Sprint I.)
\item \S 6 --- the four delegation constructions side-by-side:
  Aff$(\mathbb{R})$ algebraic capability delegation (Sprint J;
  trusted-delegatee, $O(1)$ algebraic recovery on collusion;
  intended for honest-delegatee deployments where Bob's key is
  independently protected), BLS12-381 pairing-based delegation
  (Sprint J.2; bearer-capability with $rk$-unforgeability under
  an exponent-ratio assumption in $G_2$, pre-quantum),
  ML-KEM-768 trusted-delegatee (Sprint J.3; PQ
  analogue of J.2 under MLWE), and the lattice threshold mode
  (Sprint J.4; Shamir $K$-of-$N$ composed with per-share ML-KEM
  transport, information-theoretic collusion-resistance up to
  $K{-}1$ shareholders).
\item \S 7 --- Holonomy Ledger: append-only RFC 6962 Merkle tree
  over per-write leaves carrying $(\Delta \oint \Gamma,\,
  \text{record\_hash})$. (Sprint K.)
\item \S 8 --- \v{C}ech threshold sharing: Shamir $K$-of-$N$ over
  $\mathbb{F}_p$ with pubkey-bound share tags. (Sprint L.)
\item \S 9 --- Continuous RG-flow ratchet for per-write forward
  secrecy via HKDF chain. (Sprint M.)
\item \S 10 --- Public deterministic verification of the invariant
  fingerprint $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ from ciphertext alone: an
  auditor holding only the encrypted bundle recomputes $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$
  and compares to the prover's claim, returning the first
  disagreement in canonical fingerprint order. No gauge key given
  to the verifier. (Sprint N.)
\item \S 11 --- Credential-gated invariant queries: HMAC-bound
  query-class capability tags with a runtime falsification harness
  that re-evaluates ad-hoc query callbacks under five random
  gauges and rejects anything not in the invariant ring
  $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Aff}}$. (Sprint O.)
\item \S 12 --- Geodesic-ball membership index: dimension-aware
  Mahalanobis-distance index with $\chi^2(k, 1-\alpha)$ threshold
  (exact table for $k \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$ at $p \in \{0.95, 0.99\}$
  with Wilson--Hilferty cubic fallback elsewhere); scalar-gauge
  ball preserved, per-field gauge handled via induced ellipsoidal
  Mahalanobis metric. (Sprint P.)
\item \S 13 --- $K$-preserving group characterization: the diagonal
  affine group
  $G_{\mathrm{AffK}} = (\mathbb{R}^*)^k \ltimes \mathbb{R}^k$ is
  the exact transformation group that preserves per-field
  curvature $K$. Shear matrices break $K$ (off-diagonal mixing);
  rotations preserve $\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Cov})$ but break
  $(\max - \min)^2$, so an $O(k)$-invariant trace-$K$ must use
  squared diameter rather than coordinate range. (Sprint Q.)
\item \S 14 --- empirical evaluation across the implemented suites.
\item \S 15 --- the three deferred constructions (spectral-signature
  ZKP, lattice-fiber PQ gauge, full Sigma-protocol upgrade of the
  Sprint N verifier).
\item \S\S 16--18 --- related work, limits, conclusion.
\end{itemize}

The thread tying them together is
Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance}: each construction either
\emph{preserves} a gauge invariant under some operation
(Curvature-MAC, capability delegation, ledger telescope), exploits
a non-trivial \emph{equivariance} for ciphertext-side computation
with closed-form client recovery (analytical SQL helpers
\S\,\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:examples}), or \emph{advances the
gauge} in a controlled way (RG-flow ratchet). The taxonomy of
what's possible flows directly from the choice of structure group
and the representation $\rho$ each query realises. Every
construction listed above has a passing reference implementation
and test suite in the public GIGI repository~\cite{gigi2026code}.

\appendix
\section{Worked examples (Alice and Bob)}
\label{sec:appendix:examples}
%% ==================================================================

This appendix walks through twelve concrete scenarios that
exercise the framework end-to-end. Throughout, \emph{Alice} is
the data owner (holds the gauge key), \emph{Bob} is the server
(holds only ciphertext), \emph{Carol} is an analyst or auditor
without the gauge key, and \emph{Eve} is the adversary. All
numerical values are chosen for arithmetic clarity, not
realism; the implementations in \cite{gigi2026code} use
production-strength parameters throughout.

\subsection*{A.1\quad SUM under Affine}
Alice has a three-record column of ages
$v = (47, 62, 35)$ and an Affine gauge $g = (a, b) = (2.5, 7)$.
She encrypts pointwise: $\mathrm{Enc}_g(v) = (124.5, 162.0, 94.5)$
and ships the ciphertext to Bob. Bob computes the encrypted-side
sum $\hat{s} = 124.5 + 162.0 + 94.5 = 381.0$ and returns the
single scalar. Alice recovers the plaintext sum via
$\rho_g^{-1}(\hat{s}) = (\hat{s} - nb)/a = (381.0 - 3 \cdot 7)/2.5
= 144.0$, matching the plaintext sum $47 + 62 + 35 = 144$.
Bob never sees the plaintext ages; Alice's work is one division
and one subtraction, independent of $n$.

\subsection*{A.2\quad MEDIAN under Affine (sign-aware)}
Same column. Plaintext median is $47$. With $a > 0$ (gauge
$(2.5, 7)$) the sorted ciphertext is $(94.5, 124.5, 162.0)$, so
the encrypted-side median is $124.5$; Alice recovers
$(124.5 - 7)/2.5 = 47$. With $a < 0$ (gauge $(-2.5, 7)$) the
ciphertext becomes $(-110.5, -148.0, -80.5)$; sorted, the
encrypted-side median is $-110.5$; Alice recovers
$(-110.5 - 7)/(-2.5) = 47$. The median is sign-symmetric because
the $q = \tfrac{1}{2}$ quantile is its own complement under
$q \mapsto 1 - q$; no client-side flip is needed.

\subsection*{A.3\quad ARGMIN / ARGMAX under Affine (joint swap)}
Same column $v = (47, 62, 35)$, indexed $0, 1, 2$. The plaintext
$\textsc{argmin}$ is index $2$ (value $35$) and the plaintext
$\textsc{argmax}$ is index $1$ (value $62$). Under $a > 0$ the
encrypted ranking matches: Bob returns $(\textsc{argmin}_{\text{enc}},
\textsc{argmax}_{\text{enc}}) = (2, 1)$, and Alice accepts the pair
unchanged. Under $a < 0$ (gauge $(-2.5, 7)$, ciphertext
$(-110.5, -148.0, -80.5)$), the encrypted-side
$\textsc{argmin}$ is index $1$ (value $-148.0$) and the
encrypted-side $\textsc{argmax}$ is index $2$ (value $-80.5$).
Bob returns $(1, 2)$. Alice's client detects $a < 0$ and applies
the joint swap on the answer space
$Y = [\tau] \times [\tau]$, recovering
$(\textsc{argmin}_{\text{plain}}, \textsc{argmax}_{\text{plain}})
= (2, 1)$. The swap is the action $\rho_g$ on the index-pair
answer space (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivariance}); no decryption of
fiber values is needed.

\subsection*{A.4\quad VAR under Probabilistic (bias correction)}
Alice declares a Probabilistic gauge $(a, b, \sigma) = (1.0, 0,
2.0)$ on the same column with $n = 3$ records and plaintext
(population) variance $\mathrm{Var}(v) = 122$. Bob computes the
encrypted-side sample variance estimator on the noised
ciphertexts. Under the unbiased (Bessel-corrected) convention,
its expected value is
$\mathbb{E}[\widehat{V}] = a^2 \mathrm{Var}(v) + \sigma^2$ for
the population, with a finite-sample correction of $(n{-}1)/n$
on the noise term in the small-$n$ regime; for moderate or
large $n$ this approaches $a^2 V + \sigma^2$ exactly. Treating
the formula as asymptotic, Alice recovers
$V \approx (\widehat{V} - \sigma^2) / a^2$: for example, an
observed $\widehat{V} = 126$ gives $V = 122$. The correction
works because $\mathrm{Var}$ is translation-free, so the
$(a, b)$ part of the gauge contributes only $a^2$, and the
Gaussian noise variance is additive and known to Alice.

\subsection*{A.5\quad MIN under Probabilistic (typed refusal)}
Same Probabilistic gauge with $\sigma = 2.0$. Bob computes the
encrypted-side $\textsc{min}$ over the $n$ noised values; its
expected value depends on the plaintext spread. \emph{For
widely-spread plaintexts} like our $(47, 62, 35)$ (range
$27 \gg \sigma$), the noised minimum is dominated by the
smallest record's noise term and the expected bias is near
zero. \emph{For tightly-packed plaintexts} (worst case: all
$n$ values equal), the noised minimum is the order statistic
of $n$ iid Gaussians, and its expected downward bias from the
plaintext is the standard sub-Gaussian extreme-value bound
$\Theta(\sigma \sqrt{2 \log n} / |a|)$ --- approximately
$2.96$ at $\sigma = 2, n = 3$ --- a bias that does \emph{not}
vanish as $n \to \infty$. Because the helper cannot tell which
regime applies without inspecting plaintext, it refuses any
$\textsc{min}$ call on $\sigma > 0$ by default, returning the
typed error \texttt{BiasedUnderProbabilisticNoise} rather than
silently risking a downward bias. Alice's options are
documented at the API: (i) set $\sigma = 0$ on the column
(Affine) for exact extremes, (ii) call
\texttt{decrypt\_min\_unchecked} if she accepts the bias for a
coarse bound, or (iii) pair GIGI with FHE on the single column
where exact extremes under noise are required.

\subsection*{A.6\quad Equality search under INDEXED}
Alice has a column of high-cardinality user IDs. She wants
\texttt{WHERE user\_id = 'P042'}. At ingest time each row's
\texttt{user\_id} was encrypted as $\mathrm{PRF}_k(\texttt{`P042'})
= X_{42}$, a $128$-bit ciphertext. To query, Alice (who holds the
key) computes $\mathrm{PRF}_k(\texttt{`P042'}) = X_{42}$ locally
and sends $X_{42}$ to Bob. Bob looks up $X_{42}$ in the bitmap
index and returns the matching record IDs. Bob never sees
\texttt{`P042'}. Spurious-collision probability is birthday-bounded
by $O(q^2/2^{128})$, negligible for any practical $q$. As noted
in Remark~\ref{rmk:indexed-eq}, the final tag-comparison primitive
is a fixed-width integer comparison with no data-dependent byte
loop, though the index lookup itself retains the usual
access-pattern leakage.

\subsection*{A.7\quad Bucket equality under PROBABILISTIC (Davis Identity)}
Alice stores GPS coordinates in a Probabilistic-mode column with
$\sigma = 10^{-4}$ degrees ($\approx 11$\,m) and bucket width
$w = 10^{-3}$ degrees ($\approx 110$\,m). Two records have plain
coordinates $r_1 = 37.7749$ and $r_2 = 37.7750$ (plaintext
distance $\approx 11$\,m). Their noised representations land in
the same bucket with high probability, so the Davis-Identity
predicate $\mathrm{bucket}_\sigma(r_1) =
\mathrm{bucket}_\sigma(r_2)$ returns ``equal.'' For two records
at plain distance $0.0015^\circ$ ($\approx 165$\,m), the noisy
separation $\Delta + Z$ with $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2\sigma^2)$
puts them in different buckets with high probability. The
boundary-FN rate is bounded by Proposition~\ref{prop:bucket-fp-fn}.

\subsection*{A.8\quad PROJECT INVARIANT curvature (no gauge key)}
Carol is an analyst with no gauge key but read access to the
encrypted bundle. She issues the query
\verb|PROJECT INVARIANT (curvature, capacity(0.1)) FROM patients|.
Bob computes
$K = \mathrm{Var}/\mathrm{range}^2$ and $C = \tau / K$ on the
encrypted column. Because both $K$ and $C$ are gauge-invariant
under $\mathrm{Aff}(\mathbb{R})$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-invariants}), Bob's results are
bit-identical to what plaintext would yield. Carol receives the
two numbers without any fiber-value ever being decrypted.
Attempting \verb|PROJECT INVARIANT (sum)| would fail at parse
time --- $\textsc{sum}$ is not a member of the certified
invariant-ring grammar (\S\ref{sec:gauge-invariance:ring}).

\subsection*{A.9\quad BLS12-381 bearer-capability delegation}
Alice holds BLS12-381 keys $(sk_A, pk_A = g_2^{sk_A})$. Bob
holds $(sk_B, pk_B = g_2^{sk_B})$. To delegate decryption of a
single capsule, Alice samples $r \in \mathbb{F}_p$, computes the
capsule $C_1 = g_1^r \in G_1$ and the delegation token
$rk_{A \to B} = pk_B^{1/sk_A} = g_2^{sk_B/sk_A} \in G_2$, and
transmits $rk$ to Bob over a \emph{confidential and authenticated}
channel. Bob computes the shared key by pairing:
$K_{\mathrm{shared}} = e(C_1, rk) = e(g_1, g_2)^{r \cdot sk_B/sk_A}
\in G_T$, then derives an AEAD key via HKDF and decrypts.
Honest scope: this is a bearer capability. If Eve compromises
the proxy and obtains both $rk$ and a visible $C_1$, she
computes $K_{\mathrm{shared}}$ directly via the public pairing
--- no hardness assumption protects against this. The
construction is appropriate when Alice trusts Bob (and any
proxy Bob chooses) not to leak $rk$.

\subsection*{A.10\quad Lattice $K$-of-$N$ threshold delegation}
Alice has a $32$-byte AEAD master key $K$ for an encrypted
bundle and wants to delegate decryption to a quorum of $3$ out
of $5$ deputies. She runs Shamir $K{=}3$-of-$N{=}5$ over the
secp256k1 base field $\mathbb{F}_p$, producing shares
$s_1, \ldots, s_5$ such that any $3$ shares reconstruct $K$ and
any $2$ reveal information-theoretically nothing. Each share is
then wrapped under the matching deputy's ML-KEM-768 public key:
$\mathrm{envelope}_i = \mathrm{MLKEM}.\mathrm{Encaps}(pk_i, s_i)$.
Eve compromises two deputies (say $i = 1, 2$) and recovers
$s_1, s_2$: she learns zero bits of $K$ by Shamir's
information-theoretic guarantee. When three honest deputies
(say $i = 3, 4, 5$) cooperate, each decapsulates its share
under its $sk_i$, and they jointly compute Lagrange
interpolation at zero, $K = \lambda_3 s_3 + \lambda_4 s_4 +
\lambda_5 s_5 \pmod{p}$. The outer ML-KEM layer adds NIST
Level 3 post-quantum confidentiality on each share's transport.

\subsection*{A.11\quad Public deterministic verification of $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$}
Bob the data steward publishes the encrypted bundle along with
his claim $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}^{\text{claim}} = (K = 0.142,
\lambda_1 = 1.83, \overline{\mathrm{Hol}} = 0.667, \tau = 40,
\beta_0 = 2, \beta_1 = 2)$. Carol the auditor holds no gauge
key. She recomputes $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ from the ciphertext
alone --- all six components are gauge-invariant under Affine,
so her computed values must match the plaintext fingerprint,
which equals Bob's claim. Carol observes $(0.142, 1.83, 0.667,
40, 2, 2)$ within the $10^{-10}$ quantization grain on f64
fields and exact equality on integer fields: \emph{Verified}.
Had Bob (or an adversary) tampered with a record so that the
recomputed $K$ became $0.151$, Carol would report
$\textsc{Rejected}(K, \mathrm{gap} = 0.009)$, naming the first
disagreement in canonical fingerprint order.

\subsection*{A.12\quad Curvature-MAC tamper detection}
Alice computes $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}$ on her bundle at write time
and HMACs the canonical-byte encoding under an integrity key:
$t = \mathrm{HMAC\text{-}SHA256}_{k_{\text{int}}}
(\mathrm{canon}(\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}))$. She stores
$(\text{encrypted\_bundle}, t)$ on Bob. Eve flips bits in record
$7$'s age field. At verification time, Alice (or any reader with
$k_{\text{int}}$) recomputes $\pi_{\mathrm{inv}}'$ on the
modified bundle and the tag candidate $t'$. If Eve's edit
crossed the $10^{-10}$ quantization grain on any invariant
component, $t' \neq t$ and the tamper is detected. If Eve's edit
was below the grain (a sub-quantization perturbation that left
all six components fixed), the Curvature-MAC tag misses it ---
which is why the Holonomy ledger (companion preprint \S 7) signs
per-record \texttt{record\_hash} leaves in parallel, closing the
sub-grain gap at byte level.
